**⭐ 🔹 Hugging Face's "smolagents" Definition of AI Agents**
→ Despite the ambiguity in the broader industry, the instructor points to a crisp, simple, and effective definition provided by Hugging Face in their "smolagents" project: *"AI Agents are programs where LLM outputs control the workflow."* 
This means that a single output generated by a Large Language Model (LLM) possesses the decision-making power to determine exactly what tasks are carried out and in what specific sequence. Instead of a hardcoded script dictating the next step, the model's output *is* the controller. 
> ⭐ **EXAM NOTE:** This is a highly testable, foundational definition. If asked to define an AI Agent in a strict sense, you must identify that it is a program where the LLM's output dynamically controls the workflow and sequence of execution.

**⭐ 🔹 The 5 Hallmarks of Agentic AI**
→ Because the strict definition doesn't fit every situation, the industry more broadly applies the term "Agentic AI" or "Agents" to solutions that exhibit any one of the following five hallmarks. An AI solution involves "Agentic" properties if it includes any of these features:

1. **Multiple LLM Calls:** Any system architecture that chains together or involves multiple calls to an LLM (such as the basic application built on Day 1 of the course).
2. **LLMs with the Ability to Use Tools:** This is often considered the ultimate "litmus test" for whether something is truly an agent. It refers to giving an LLM the capacity to interact with external systems or APIs. 
   * *Analogy/Example:* The instructor references a Day 1 demonstration using an **n8n** workflow, where an LLM was given tool access that allowed it to literally turn on the physical lights the instructor was wearing.
3. **An Environment Where LLMs Interact:** A system setup—acting as a coordination or orchestration environment—where multiple different LLMs communicate and send information to one another to solve a problem.
4. **A Planner to Coordinate Activities:** The presence of a dedicated process (which is itself an LLM) that acts as a "planner" to oversee, orchestrate, and coordinate a sequence of activities.
5. **Autonomy:** (Detailed extensively as its own standalone concept below).

> ⭐ **EXAM NOTE:** You must memorize these 5 hallmarks. Questions may ask you to identify which feature makes a standard script "agentic." Tool usage, multiple LLM interactions, and planners are key indicators of an agentic architecture.

**⭐ 🔹 Autonomy in Agentic AI**
→ Autonomy is the 5th hallmark of Agentic AI, but the instructor emphasizes that it truly captures the "essence" of what makes Agentic AI unique. Autonomy means giving an LLM the ability to control the order in which things happen. It is effectively allowing the LLM to "choose its own adventure." By granting it autonomy, you give the LLM the agency to decide how it will carry out future actions without human intervention.
* *Example 1 (Business Sector Project):* The instructor references a previous challenge project where an LLM was tasked with coming up with a business sector on its own and then analyzing it to identify pain points. Because the LLM chose the sector itself and then executed the analysis based on its own choice, it demonstrated autonomy.
* *Example 2 (Q&A):* At a simpler level, asking a question and letting the LLM independently formulate the steps to answer it is a form of autonomy. 
The instructor notes that while the word "autonomous" can sound "spooky" (implying human-like consciousness), it is ultimately just a fancy use of language to describe autonomous statistical language models running iteratively.
> ⭐ **EXAM NOTE:** Autonomy is the defining characteristic that separates static workflows from dynamic agents. Understand that autonomy in this context simply means the model dictates the sequence and selection of future actions dynamically.

**⭐ 🔹 Anthropic's "Agentic Systems" Framework (Workflows vs. Agents)**
→ To go one level deeper into agentic theory, the instructor cites a "brilliant" and "highly clear" blog post by Anthropic titled **"Building Effective Agents"**. In this post, Anthropic introduces the umbrella term **"Agentic Systems"** and distinctly divides it into two sub-categories based on how control is handled:

| **Workflows** | **Agents** |
| :--- | :--- |
| Systems where LLMs and tools are orchestrated through **predefined code paths**. | Systems where LLMs **dynamically direct** their own processes and tool usage. |
| The developer dictates the exact flow of execution (e.g., Step A -> Step B -> Step C). | The LLM maintains control over *how* tasks get accomplished, adapting on the fly. |
| According to Anthropic, many systems people loosely call "agents" are actually just workflows. | This is the truer, stricter definition of an autonomous agent making its own routing decisions. |

The instructor points out the irony (and wordplay) in the industry: even though Anthropic clarifies that many so-called "agents" are technically just "workflows," both categories still fall under the broader, accepted umbrella term of "Agentic Systems." 
> ⭐ **EXAM NOTE:** The distinction between Workflows (predefined paths) and Agents (dynamically LLM-directed paths) as defined by Anthropic is critical for architecture questions. You will likely be tested on classifying a system as a Workflow or an Agent based on who or what dictates the path of execution.
